Baroque

The Baroque is a style that dominated architecture, painting, literature, and music in 17th century Europe. For this reason, the entire culture of this period, including customs, values, and social relations, is called "baroque. This period emerged at the end of the Renaissance and manifested itself through great ostentation and extravagance among the groups that benefited from the riches of colonization.

Characteristics

The main characteristics of the Baroque, also called sixteenthism, are dualism, richness of detail, and exaggeration. In Baroque literature, the texts reflect far-fetched and almost always extravagant elements, where details are valued in a game of contrasts.

  • Far-fetched and exaggerated art;
  • Appreciation of detail;
  • Dualism and contradictions;
  • Obscurity, complexity, and sensuality;
  • Literary Baroque: cultism and conceptism.

Major Works and its Artists

The Emmaus Supper (1601), Caravaggio

Characterized by the rudeness of his works, Caravaggio painted religious themes where he explored the contrast between light and shadows. Highlights include: "The Takingen of Christ", "Flagellation of Christ", "The Death of the Virgin", "The Supper at Emmaus", "David with Goliath's Head", "Flagellation of Christ".

The Ecstasy of Saint Theresa (1647-1652) Bernini

Bernini was an Italian sculptor and architect. His works can be found in Rome and the Vatican, among them: "Saint Peter's Square", "Saint Peter's Cathedral", "The Ecstasy of Saint Theresa", "Bust of Paul V", and "Saint Angelo's Castle".
