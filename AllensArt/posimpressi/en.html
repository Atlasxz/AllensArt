Post-Impressionism

Post-impressionism was a trend in the arts that occurred in France in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. This innovative movement began to emerge in 1880 and remained until the emergence of Cubism, in 1907. In fact, this current organizes itself spontaneously, inspiring and at the same time confronting the so-called impressionism. The term post-impressionism was first used by British art critic Roger Eliot Fry (1866-1934), to designate the works exhibited at the Grafton Galleries in London in 1910.

Characteristics

The post-impressionist current was not homogeneous, and in the same way, the art produced in this period has several characteristics. However, we can list some particularities in the works of the artists who fit into this trend. To wit:

  • Subjectivism
  • Chromatic freedom;
  • Appreciation of light and texture;;
  • Pointillist technique;
  • Valuing themes from everyday life;
  • Two-dimensionality to the detriment of perspective.

Major Works and its Artists

The Starry Night (1889) - Vincent Van Gogh

Van Gogh (1853-1890) was a Dutch painter of the late 19th century and one of the greatest exponents of post-impressionism. Main Works:

  • Bedroom in Arles (1888)
  • The Starry Night (1889)
  • The Sunflowers (1889)

A Sunday afternoon on the island of Grande Jatte (1884-86) - Georges-Pierre Seurat

Georges Seurat was an important French painter, considered the pioneer of Pointillism. Like Cézanne, Seurat is also known as one of the representatives of the post-impressionist movement (Neoimpressionism). One of his best known works, A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of Grande Jatte, changed the direction of modern art and was the starting point of Neoimpressionism. Moreover, she is still regarded as one of the icons of 19th century painting. Among her works are:

  • A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of Grande Jatte, George Seurat (1884).
  • The Models, George Seurat (1887)
  • The Great Stroll on the River Seine, George Seurat (1888)
